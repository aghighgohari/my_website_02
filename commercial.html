The Industrial Age  ·  1850–1914

In the second half of the 19th century, Persian rugs discovered a new patron: the Western drawing room. As industrial wealth expanded the European and American middle classes, a taste for the exotic — for the handmade, the ancient, the far-away — made Persian rugs into status symbols across London, Paris, and New York.

Workshops in Tabriz, Isfahan, and Kashan adapted quickly. Designs softened. Medallions became less dense, colors were muted to suit Victorian interiors, and floral patterns grew more naturalistic to appeal to European taste.

The carpet was being translated — not just sold, but rewritten for a new audience.

✦   ✦   ✦

Trading houses — most notably Ziegler & Co. of Manchester — opened direct partnerships with Persian workshops, standardizing production and introducing synthetic dyes imported from Germany. The efficiency was remarkable. The cultural cost was real.

This era marks the first tension between craft and commerce that would define the century to come — a tension the modern revival would eventually have to resolve.
